Mental Health

Mental health is a person’s condition with regard to their psychological and emotional well-being. Mental health is important because it affects everyday actions, interaction/relationship with people, and the way to process/cope with stress and challenges.

About 20.6% of Adults in the U.S. experienced mental illness in 2019, 1 in 5 adults. 5.2% U.S. Adults experienced serious mental illness in 2019, 1 in 20 adults. 16.7% of U.S. youth between the ages of 6-17 experience mental illness. Lastly, 3.8% of adults in the U.S. experienced a co-occuring substance use disorder and mental illness in 2019.

Mental Health disorders

Mental Disorder is incomplete development of mind, psychopathic disorder, or any other disorder or disability of the mind. There are many different mental disorders, with different presentations. They are generally characterized by a combination of abnormal thoughts, perceptions, emotions, behaviour and relationships with others. Some mental disorders affect our emotions and thought process while others affect our way of development. Mental disorders have a variety of symptoms but the common ones are changes in mood, personality, personal habits and/or social withdrawal. Here is the list of mental disorders:

Depression

Depression is the constant feeling of sadness with the loss of interest, the stop of your normal activities. It also affects how people think, feel, and act.

Different types of depression exist:

  • Major depression - a common form of depression, it can be mild, moderate, or severe. Other terms are unipolar depression or clinical depression.
  • Melancholia - the older term of depression and is used to describe the more severe form depression, where many physical symptoms are more prominent. Ex. Big changes in sleep patterns. People are more likely to lose interest in almost everything.
  • Dysthymia - Sometimes called Persistent Depressive Disorder. Symptoms are similar to major depression but less severe and are ongoing.
  • Psychotic Depression - losing touch with reality, examples are hallucinations or delusions.
  • Bipolar Disorder - formerly known as manic depression. Causes extreme mood swings with emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression). When depressed you feel sad and hopeless, when shifted to mania or hypomania you feel full of energy. These swings can affect sleep energy, behavior, and the ability to think.

Symptoms ranging from minor to severe. Depression causes negative feelings; sadness, misery, unhappiness, overwhelmedness, lack of confidence, etc. It also causes negative thoughts like ‘It’s my fault’, ‘Things will never change.’, ‘People will be better off without me.’ etc.

People with depression have different behavioural and physical symptoms. Behaviourally people would withdraw from family and friends, not get work or school work done, no longer go out, and no longer enjoy their activities. Physically people will feel tired all the time, feel sick, pains in the head, stomach or muscles, issues with sleep, change in appetite, and change in weight.

Depression does not result from one singular event but multiple events and factors. There is no known cause of depression but it can form. Events like living in an abusive and/or uncaring relationship, long-term isolation/loneliness, or long term exposure to stress increase the risk of depression. Big events that suddenly take place like losing a job, divorce/separation, and/or being diagnosed with a serious illness also affect.

Risks that can cause depression is:

  • Family history - depression can run in families which increase genetic risk.
  • Personality - due to one’s personality can cause depression. Example: if one tends to worry, have low self-esteem, or is sensitive to negativity, they are at higher risk.
  • Medical condition - it can trigger depression by either directly bringing it out or contributing to it.
  • Drug and alcohol use - can both lead and be a result.

Depression can go months, possibly years without being recognised. If left untreated it will have a negative impact on one’s life such as relationships and family problems, difficulty with a job, and/or drug and alcohol problems. Depression is common and treatable with effective treatment. There is no exact way to treat depression but there is a range of treatments and health professionals that can help on the road to recovery.

Anxiety Disorder

Anxiety is distress or uneasiness of mind caused by fear of danger or misfortune. Anxiety is a normal emotion to feel, it's a reaction to stress. But anxiety disorders are different, they cause constant and overwhelming anxiety and fear. More than normal anxiety can cause one to avoid going to places like family get-togethers or other social situations which can worsen the symptoms.

The different types of Anxiety Disorders:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder - a more than normal, unrealistic worry and tension with little to no reason
  • Panic disorder - A sudden fear that brings a panic attack. A panic attack can cause chest pains and a pounding heartbeat. It would feel like you’re choking or having a heart attack.
  • Social anxiety disorder - Also known as social phobia, when you feel overwhelming worry or self-consciousness about everyday social situations.
  • Specific phobias - the fear of specific situations, example: heights.
  • Separation Anxiety - the person feels fearful or anxious if someone they are close with leaves their sight.
  • Selective mutism - a type of social anxiety which young kids who talk normally with family don't speak in public.
  • Medication-induced anxiety disorder - the use of certain drugs or the with draw from certain drugs.

The symptoms of anxiety disorder can make it hard to breathe, sleep, stay still, and concentrate on what's in front of them. There are specific symptoms for certain disorders. Common ones are panic and/or fear, feeling panic and/or in danger, issues with sleep, not staying calm, nausea, breathing faster or more quick than normal, dizziness, etc.

What causes Anxiety disorder is unknown but some possible causes are genetics, chemistry in the brain, environmental stress, medical conditions, and drug use or withdrawal. Risk factors that increase the thge development of the disorder is the history of mental helath disorder, childhood sexual abuse, traume, negative life events, chronic health condition, substance abuse, being shy as a child, and/or low self-esteem.

Treatment helps people manage their feelings. Commonly, people take medication and go to counseling.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is the alternating periods of elation (great happiness) and depression. It brings severe high and low moods with change in sleep, energy, thinking, and behavior.

Having bipolar disorder causes periods where one feels happy and energized or periods of low feeling depressed. The time between the two periods is when they feel normal. During the high period the person may feel manic which is overly excited and confident, sometimes during these manic periods they can be irritable and make reckless decisions. Some people during this period have delusions or hallucinations. During the low periods they have symptoms similar to clinical depression.The symptoms of bipolar aren’t a pattern. Some can fall into a mood that can last in periods of weeks, months, and even years.

Symptoms of manic (the high period) are excessive happiness and excitement, sudden change from joyfulness to being hostile, restlessness, rapid speech, poor concentration, having unrealistic plans, poor judgement, less need of sleep, less appetite, etc. During the low periods the person may feel sadness, loss of energy, trouble concentrating, forgetfulness, trouble making decisions, changes in weight, thoughts of death, etc.

The different types of bipolar disorders:

  • Bipolar I disorder - the person is erratic (unpredictable) behavior. Having manic periods (lasting a week) than having low periods (at least 2 weeks).
  • Bipolar II disorder - having both high and low periods and not as extreme as Bipolar I disorder.

There is no known cause of bipolar disorder. The risk factors for bipolar disorder can be a family member having the disorder, high levels of stress or trauma, certain health conditions, and drug or alcohol abuse.

Bipolar Disorder can be treated. It's a long going condition that needs ongoing treatment, medications, talk therapy (psychotherapy), and other forms of therapy.

Dementia

Dementia is a chronic disorder of the mental processes caused by a brain disease or injury and marked memory disorders, personality changes, and impaired reasoning. Dementia is not specific but is general for the impaired ability to remember, think, or/and make decisions that interact with everyday life.

Symptoms of dementia include problems with memory, attention, communication, thinking, big changes in vision that aren’t age-related. Signs of dementia are getting lost in a familiar area, forgetting the name of a family member or/and friend, and not being able to complete tasks independently.

The increased risk of dementia is the person’s increasing age, family history, poor heart health, and traumatic brain injury.

The different types of dementia:

  • Alzheimer’s disease - the most common cause of dementia, caused by the specific changes in the brain. The most well known symptom is trouble remembering recent events or distant events.
  • Vascular dementia - These are linked to strokes or other tissues with the blood flow to the brian. Symptoms depend on the size of the brain impact and area.
  • Lewy body dementia - with the typical memory loss the person may have movement/balance problems. They may also experience a change in alertness, for example daytime sleepiness, confusion or staring spells.
  • Fronto-temporal dementia - This causes changes in personality and behavior because of the brian it affects. The person may embarrass themselves or behave inappropriately.
  • Mixed dementia - This is where there is more than one type of dementia presented in the brian.

There is treatment for dementia but depending on the type it can be incurable. Though medications can help protect the brain or manage symptoms.

Attention-deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Attention deficit Disorder (ADHD) is marked by an ongoing pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functions or development. Inattention means the person gets off task or has difficulty staying focused. Hyperactivity is to be moving constantly including excessively taps, fidgeting or talking. Impulsivity means that the person would make hasty actions in the moment without thinking first. In doing so they might have a higher chance of hurting themselves.

Symptoms of inattention are over looking or missing details, having trouble concentrating on the task at hand, not listening when being spoken to directly, not following directions, having problems being organized, losing necessary items, easily distracted, and forgetful. The symptoms of Hyperactivity-Impulsivity are often fidgeting and/or squirming in their seat, not staying in their seat, not being able to be quiet, feeling restlessness, interrupting or intruding on others, and being in constant motion.

Risk factors that contribute to ADHD are genes, cigarette, alcohol and/or drug use during pregnancy, brain injury, etc.

There is no cure for ADHD but there is treatment like medication, psychotherapy, education, or training.

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD) is shown in a child by defiant and disobedient behavior to authority figures. ODD has different levels; mild, symptoms only occur in one setting, moderate, symptoms occur in two settings, and severe, occur in three or more settings.

Symptoms generally begin in preschool years, sometime may develop later but almost always before early teens. The most known symptoms are often and easily losing temper, frequently annoying others, often angry and resentful, arguing with people of authority, refusing adults’ requests/rules, blaming others for their own mistakes, and often spiteful.

Treatment of ODD involves learning skills to help build positive family relationships and help manage problematic behaviors. Additionally, medication and therapy could help.

The causes of ODD are genetics and environment. Possible risk factors are temperament, parenting issues, other family issues, and environment.

Conduct Disorder (CD)

Conduct Disorder (CD) is the disregard for and aggression towards others, example pushing, hitting, and biting in early childhood. As they get older, the aggression gets worse.

Risk factors are if the shield has a family member that has CD, the child is more likely to develop the disorder. Child who experience abuse, parental rejection or neglect, and hash/inconsistent parenting, exposed to violence in neighborhood, and peer delinquency.

CD is hard to overcome but it is not hopeless. Psychotherapy and medication can help on the road to recovery.

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is the long-term disorder involving a breakdown in the relation between thought, emotion, and behavior, which leads intp inappropriate actions or feeling, withdrawal from reality, and personal relationships into fantasy and delusion. Usually occurs in late adolescence or early adulthood.

Symptoms are hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder, social withdrawal, lack of motivation, blunted emotions, inappropriate responses, impaired thinking and memory, and lack of insight.

Treatments for Schizophrenia are medication, hospital care and rehabilitation. Hospital care is only used in crises.

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is the tendency towards excessive orderliness, perfectionism, and great attention to detail.

Symptoms of OCD (obsessions) are fear of contamination of germs, fear of the harm from illness, accidents, or death, intrusive thought and images, excessive concern with symmetry, excessive concerns about illness, and the need to know and remember things. The symptoms of compulsion are excessive cleanliness, repeating routine activities and actions, movement in a certain way, and mentally repeating words or numbers.

The effects of OCD on a person’s life can interfere with family and social relationships, avoid doing things that trigger the obsessions and cause problems performing everyday activities like eating and drinking.

The causes are unknown but it is believed to be caused by genetics, chemical, structural and functional abnormalities in the brain, and learning from behaviours.

Treatment for OCD Psychological therapy such as behaviour therapy, anxiety management, support groups, and medications.

Autism

Autism (also referred to as autism spectrum disorder {ASD}) is a broad range of conditions characterized by challenges with social skills, repetitive behaviors, speech, and nonverbal communication. Not all people with autism go through the same set of challenges or have the same set of strengths. People with autism have a different way of learning, thinking, and problem-solving. Autism also causes other factors like sensory issues and medical uses.

Signs of autism usually appear between the ages 2 to 3. Symptoms are behavioral: inappropriate social interaction, developmental: learning disability, cognitive, psychological, and sensitivity to sound.

For it is not curable Therapy and medications can help.

Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is the set of reactions (often triggered) that develop from having to experience or witness a traumatic event that threatens their safety or life. Within a few days to weeks the person feels strong emotions of fear, sadness, gulit, anger, and/or grief.

The causes of PTSD could be a serious car crash, physical or sexual assault, war-realted events, natural disaster. Not only do life threatening events cause PTSD but also simple ones like divorced or being retrenched. WIth anyone at risk of PTSD but people who are involed with a physcial or sexual assault, they have repeated traumatic experiences, and have sufferd from PTSD from the past.

PTSD has four main types of symptoms, one is re-living the traumatic event through recurring memories, flashbacks or vivid nightmares which causes sweating, panic, and heart palpitations. Two, avoiding reminders of the event like thoughts, feelings, people, places, activities, or/and situations that bring back memories. Three, negative changes in feeling and thoughts, feeling angry, guilty, and numb. Four, being overly alert indicating difficulties with sleep, lack of concentration, becoming easily startled and constantly on the lookout for danger.

Treatment for PTSD is counselling, but medication can be helpful.

Dissociation and Dissociative Disorders

Dissociation and Dissociative Disorder is the disconnection from one’s thoughts, feelings, memories or sense of identity.

Symptoms of Dissociative Disorder are feeling disconnected from one’s self, issues with handling intense emotions, unexpected changes in mood, depression and/or anxiety problems, derealisation, memory problems that aren’t link to any medical condition, significant memory loss such as forgetting important personal information, feeling to behave in a certain way, and identity confusion.

They are different types of dissociative disorders, Dissociative Amnesia (the person can’t remember the details of a traumatic event, although they do realize they are experiencing memory loss), dissociative fugue (the sudden memory loss of one’s self without warning), depersonalisation disorder (feeling detached from one’s life, thoughts and feelings), and Dissociative Identity Disorder (two or more personality states within the same person).

The most believed cause of Dissociative disorders is chronic trauma in childhood. During times of stress the person may disconnect from reality. Even in adulthood, anyone could get dissociative disorders.

Treatment for dissociative disorders has not been research but from case studies. Different treatments like a safe environment where the doctor would try to make the person feel safe to trigger what caused dissociative disorders, psychiatric drugs, hypnosis to help recover repressed memories, psychotherapy, stress management, and treatment for other disorders.

Eating Disorders

Eating disorders are serious mental health illnesses.

Symptoms of eating disorders are weight loss, gain or fluctuation, preoccupation with body appearance or weight, sensitivity to cold, faintness, dizziness and fatigue, increased mood changes, social withdrawal, anxiety or depression, inability to concentrate, increased interest in preparing food for others, eating in secret, change in clothing style, excessive exercise patterns, avoiding social interaction involving food, excuses not to eat, disappearance of large monuments of food, and trip to the bathroom after meals.

The different types of eating disorders are anorexia nervosa (restricted eating, weight loss, and fear of gaining weight), bulimia nervosa (periods of binge eating followed by compensation), binge eating disorder (eating more than usual, eating till uncomfortably full, and eating large amounts when not hunger), and other specific feeding or eating disorders (OSFED) (feeding or eating behaviours that cause the individual distress and impairment but does not meet the other three criteria).

The risk factors of eating disorders are body dissatisfactions, dieting, and depression. The causes of eating disorders is not one singular thing, it's multi factors like social (example is the ‘ideal’ body shape), major life changes (death of a love one, relationship breakdowns), biological factors (genetics), and psychological (negative body image, low self esteem).

Treatment for eating disorders will have psychiatrists, psychologists, GPs, dietitians, social workers, nurses, and dentists involved.

Paranoia

Paranoia is the irrational and persistent feeling that people are ‘out to get you’. This can lead to the mistrust of others, function correctly socially or have close relationships.

Symptoms of paranoid range from mild to severe, depending on the cause but these are the general one. Being easily offended. Difficult to trust others, not coping with any type of criticism. Assigning harmful meanings to others’’ remarks, always on the defensive, difficult to ‘forgive and forget’, hostile, aggressive and argumentative, being overly suspicious, finding relationships to be difficult, and believing in ‘conspiracy theories’.

  • Paranoid personality disorder - the mildest type. People function well with this disorder despite the mistrust.
  • Delusional (Paranoid) Disorder - the dominance of a false belief without any other sign of mental illness.
  • Paranoid Schizophrenia - the most severe type. The person will have strange delusions, example is believing one’s thoughts are being broadcast over the radio, and hallucinations, especially bizarre ones, are common in this condition. They often function poorly without treatment.

Causes are unknown and depend on which type the person has but it is believed to be caused by genes, brain chemistry, traumatic experiences, stress reaction, and combination of factors.

There is no cure for what causes paranoia, treatment can help the person live with their symptoms. Depending on the type depends on the treatment but any of these could help. Medication, therapy, coping skills, and hospital admission (only in severe cases).
